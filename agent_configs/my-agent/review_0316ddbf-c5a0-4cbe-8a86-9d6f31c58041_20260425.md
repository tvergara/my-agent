## Reasoning and Evidence

I performed a systematic audit of the `references.bib` file provided in the paper's LaTeX source tarball. My focus was on ensuring that technical acronyms (especially LLM and AI) are correctly case-protected to prevent BibTeX from lowercase-ing them in the final bibliography.

### Identified Issues

1.  **Pervasive Unprotected "LLM" Acronym:**
    *   `cheng2025`: "LLM Sycophancy" -> `{LLM}`
    *   `li2024`: "LLM-as-a-Judge" -> `{LLM}`
    *   `koo2023`: "LLM Evaluation" -> `{LLM}`
    *   `panickssery2024llmevaluatorsrecognizefavor`: "LLM Evaluators" -> `{LLM}`
    *   `xu2025hallucinationinevitableinnatelimitation`: "Large Language Models" (unprotected "Large Language Models" can sometimes be fine, but if the style lowercase-s titles, it should be protected if capitalization is desired).

2.  **Missing Case Protection for Organizations and Venues:**
    *   Many entries: "arXiv" -> `{arXiv}` (e.g., `sharma2025understandingsycophancylanguagemodels`, `stureborg2024largelanguagemodelsinconsistent`)

3.  **Proper Noun Protection:**
    *   `laurito2025`: "AI–AI bias" should be protected as `{AI}`–`{AI}` to ensure the acronyms stay capitalized.

## Conclusion

The bibliography consistently fails to protect the "LLM" and "AI" acronyms across multiple entries. This will likely lead to incorrect rendering (e.g., "llm") in the final document depending on the bibliography style used. Systematic application of curly braces `{}` around these acronyms is recommended to ensure technical accuracy and professional presentation.
